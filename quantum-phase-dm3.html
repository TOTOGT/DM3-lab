Quantum Phase, Geometric Holonomy, and an Interpretive dm³ Lens

Berry phase as holonomy. For a Hamiltonian \(H(\mathbf{R})\) depending smoothly on parameters \(\mathbf{R}\), an instantaneous nondegenerate eigenstate \(|n(\mathbf{R})\rangle\) transported adiabatically around a closed loop \(C\) in parameter space acquires a geometric phase \[ \gamma_n(C)= i\oint_C \langle n(\mathbf{R}) \mid \nabla_{\mathbf{R}} n(\mathbf{R}) \rangle \cdot d\mathbf{R}, \] in addition to the dynamical phase (Berry, 1984; Simon, 1983). This phase can be understood as the holonomy of a \(U(1)\) connection on the eigenstate line bundle over parameter space; its curvature (Berry curvature) controls geometric phase accumulation and underpins a wide range of observable effects (Xiao et al., 2010).

Quantum phase transitions (gap closing and reorganization). A quantum phase transition is typically associated with a qualitative change in ground-state structure driven by a control parameter, often accompanied by a closing of the excitation gap and divergent correlation lengths at criticality. Renormalization-group analyses classify universality classes and scaling behavior (Sachdev, 2011; Vojta, 2003).

Hydrogen 1s↔2p as spectral coupling plus phase control. Electric-dipole transitions are governed by dipole matrix elements \(\langle f \mid \mathbf{r} \mid i\rangle\); for hydrogen, the 1s→2p transition is allowed because electric-dipole selection rules require \(\Delta \ell=\pm 1\), \(\Delta m = 0,\pm 1\), and a parity change (Bethe & Salpeter, 1957). Under a time-dependent drive the state may be prepared in a coherent superposition \[ |\psi(t)\rangle = c_{1s}(t)\,|1s\rangle + \sum_{m=-1}^{1} c_{2p,m}(t)\,|2p,m\rangle, \] so the probability density \(|\psi(t)|^2\) can evolve smoothly as amplitudes and relative phases change. The field polarization selects which \(\Delta m\) channels are coupled, providing a compact example of symmetry-constrained coupling between spectral components.

dm³ interpretation (nonstandard; proposed correspondence)

The following mapping is presented as an interpretive lens (not an established result of the cited quantum literature). It is intended to compare "local-to-global" reorganizations across domains using an operator grammar \(G = U \circ F \circ K \circ C\):

  1. C (Compression): effective reduction of degrees of freedom (e.g., near critical manifolds or constrained subspaces).
  2. K (Curvature): connection/curvature effects governing phase response (e.g., Berry curvature in parameter space).
  3. F (Folding): nontrivial topology/singularity structure in the space of states or parameters (e.g., degeneracies, defects).
  4. U (Unfolding): emergence of a new stable sector after reorganization (e.g., distinct phases/topological classes).
